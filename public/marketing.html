The Loam Map

Four data sources.
One honest portrait of your ground.

NRCS soil science. USGS terrain. USDA hardiness. Parcel geometry. Translated into language you can actually use in a garden.

Soil profile · 0–36 inches
Your ground in cross-section

Surface · 0–8 in.

Dark, organic-rich topsoil. Good tilth. Keep protected with mulch year-round.

A horizon · 8–18 in.

Silty clay loam. Moderate drainage. Improves significantly with organic matter.

B horizon · 18–30 in.

Clay accumulation. Slow permeability. Raised beds recommended for this layer.

Subsoil · 30–36 in.

Parent material. Gravelly in places. Drainage limits at this depth.

⚠
Drainage caution: Low corner shows pooling risk after heavy rain. Delay bed work 24 hours post-precipitation.
Language mode

This ground holds water longer than average. After rain it stays wet a day or two before you can work it. That's not a flaw — it means once you get something growing, it won't dry out fast. Keep the surface covered with mulch and wait a day after heavy rain before digging.

Somewhat poorly drained silty clay loam. High water-holding capacity, moderate CEC. Classic conditions for Back to Eden or no-dig — the clay structure benefits greatly from added organic matter. Compaction risk if worked wet. Permanent mulch paths recommended.

The Rule of Life

Five movements.
One faithful week.

A Cistercian-inspired weekly rhythm rooted in ora et labora — pray and work. Adapted from 900 years of monastic land stewardship. The monks understood what we are relearning: the garden is not a project. It is a practice.

I
Observe

Notice what the land is doing without you. Watch water, sun, and life before acting.

II
Tend

Do the smallest faithful tasks that move things forward. Consistency over intensity.

III
Restrain

Say no to the urge to overbuild or overplant. The garden asks for less than you think.

IV
Receive

Enjoy what has already come into being. Pause in the abundance before it passes.

V
Record

Give the week a line in your long memory. What you don't record, time will take.

Six Philosophy Lenses

Which path fits
your land and your household?

Hortus doesn't impose a doctrine. It offers six paths — each with real gifts and real costs. Discern slowly. The land will help you choose.

🪵
Back to Eden

Heavy mulch, high microbial life, minimal disturbance. Slow to establish, forgiving to maintain. Rewards patience and material intelligence.

Low-disturbanceWood chipSoil life
🥕
No-Dig

Compost on top, permanent beds, no tilling. Preserves soil structure. Ideal for clay-heavy ground and gardeners wanting less hard labor over time.

Compost firstPermanent beds
🌀
Permaculture

Whole-system design, stacking functions, edge effects. Plant guilds, water harvesting, and long-term ecological intelligence in every decision.

Whole-systemGuilds
🌿
Kitchen Garden

Small, precise, beautiful. Herbs and greens in reach of your door. Suits busy households wanting intimate daily harvests over large-scale production.

SuccessionDaily harvest
🦋
Native Habitat

Ecological restoration, pollinator support, native plant communities. The land as habitat first, garden second. Deeply regional and deeply needed.

PollinatorsNative plants
🏡
Homestead

Utility, yield, preservation, resilience. Practical food systems, seed saving, and long-range planning. The garden as the economic and moral foundation of a household that owns its means of subsistence.

Community Resilience Layer

Inspired by Strong Towns' Strength Test and Local Futures' Localization Action Guide. Your garden is more than food — it's infrastructure.

Food Resilience Score
A 0-100 score measuring your household food security: pounds produced, varieties grown, days of food, seeds saved, crops shared. Could your community survive on local food for a month?

Production per square foot across all your beds. Strong Towns measures revenue per acre — we measure harvest per square foot. See which plots perform and which need attention.

Share seeds with your community. Offers, requests, swaps. Heirloom tracking across seasons. 90% of crop diversity is gone — every seed shared is an act of resilience.

Compost Tracker
Green/brown ratio tracking, temperature monitoring, turn reminders. The most impactful thing you can do for your soil. Closes the nutrient loop.

Identifies vacant and underutilized land near you. NRI assesses each site for sun, soil, water access, and zoning. Public land scores higher for community garden potential.

An interactive map of America showing every Hortus garden, every farmers market, CSA, seed library, and community garden. Auto-updates with every new member.
Design

Technology as a bridge to the garden, not a replacement. Gentle nudges after 10 minutes. One-tap quick logging. Auto-close after tasks. Get outside.
